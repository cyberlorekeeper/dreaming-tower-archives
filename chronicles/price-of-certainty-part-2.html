Real-World Cybersecurity Concept: Deception Technology / Honeypots / Controlled Incident Response / Stakeholder Communication During Active Crisis

Letting the Attacker Stay

By this point, the attack is no longer about breaking systems.

It is about buying time.

In cybersecurity, the strategy Cassia approves is called deception technology. Instead of trying to immediately remove an attacker, defenders create fake systems, fake data, and fake activity that looks valuable. The attacker is allowed to continue operating inside a controlled environment while real systems are quietly rebuilt behind the scenes.

This sounds reckless. It is often the only survivable option.

The concept has a long lineage. Clifford Stoll documented one of the earliest honeypot operations in 1986, tracking a West German hacker through Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory by creating fake files labeled "SDInet" to keep the intruder engaged while authorities traced the connection. The attacker spent months exploring fabricated military data. Stoll let him. The deception held long enough to identify the threat and coordinate a response across international law enforcement.

That was nearly forty years ago. The principle hasn't changed. The stakes have gotten larger.

Why Defenders Sometimes Let Attackers Stay

When an attacker has already spread through critical systems, immediate removal can be more dangerous than delay. Kicking them out too early can destroy forensic evidence needed to understand the full scope of compromise, trigger destructive retaliation (ransomware deployment, data wiping), cause corruption to spread into backup systems the attacker has already positioned against, and leave defenders blind to other footholds the attacker may have established.

So instead, sophisticated security teams sometimes isolate compromised segments, create decoy environments, feed attackers believable but fake data, and monitor behavior while rebuilding clean systems in parallel.

This is exactly what the Council does. The decoy sector is the honeypot. The Cipher Monks rebuilding behind barriers is the parallel clean infrastructure. Shade watching the entity's behavior is threat intelligence collection. And Cassia holding the merchants together on manual operations is business continuity management.

The goal is not to trap the attacker forever. The goal is to survive long enough to recover.

The Three-Day Hold: Business Continuity Under Duress

Real incident responders describe this phase as the hardest part of any breach. Not the detection. Not the technical remediation. The waiting. Cassia's three-day hold is realistic. During major incidents, business continuity teams routinely sustain manual operations for days or weeks:

  • Manufacturing: Norsk Hydro, one of the world's largest aluminum producers, operated on manual processes for weeks after a 2019 LockerGoga ransomware attack. Production continued at reduced capacity. Cost: $70M+.
  • Government: The City of Baltimore operated without email, online bill payment, and real estate transaction processing for weeks after a 2019 RobbinHood ransomware attack. Cost: $18M+ in direct costs and lost revenue.

In every case, the organizations that recovered successfully shared one trait: leadership that communicated honestly about the situation, set realistic timelines, and held the organization together through the discomfort of operating without their normal tools.

Deception strategies only work when leadership is transparent with the people who matter. You can deceive the attacker. You cannot deceive your own team and expect them to hold together through a crisis.

The technology buys time. Trust holds the organization together. Without both, the system collapses long before the attacker ever wins.

  • Know how long you can operate without your systems. If your point-of-sale system goes down, can you run on paper for a day? Three days? A week? Know the answer before the crisis. Cassia knew her merchants could handle three days. Do you know your number?
  • Communicate with your team, not just your customers. During a security incident, your employees are operating in the dark. They see systems failing. They hear rumors.   • The fix doesn't have to be instant. Three days of imperfect manual operations saved the Kingdom. Perfection wasn't required. Survival was. Give yourself permission to operate imperfectly while you work the problem.
