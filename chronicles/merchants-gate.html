The Breach at Merchant’s Gate

Trusted writs. Shadow markets. Seven intruders, coordinated like a swarm.

Theme: Supply Chain Threat: Rot Swarm Status: Complete

Flip the page to reveal the real-world meaning.

The Dreaming Tower shuddered.

It was not a sound that anyone else could hear, not the guards patrolling the Crown City's luminous streets below, not the Cipher Monks chanting their encryption rituals in the eastern sanctum, not even the Queen herself in her palace of glass and iron across the plaza. But the Lorekeeper felt it in his bones, a vibration that traveled through the ancient stones and into his flesh like a warning bell struck in another world.

He stood from his desk, knocking over a brass inkwell that spilled across a half-finished chronicle. The black liquid spread like a stain, but he paid it no mind. His eyes had already gone distant, unfocused, as the Tower's consciousness pressed against his own.

Show me, he thought, and the world around him dissolved.

The vision came in fragments, as it always did. The Tower did not speak in words or present neat explanations. It showed him patterns, flickers of wrongness stitched together from a thousand monitoring enchantments woven throughout the Kingdom.

He saw the Merchant's Gate, the southern entrance to the Crown City where trade caravans arrived daily from the Outer Provinces. Wagons laden with goods. Merchants presenting their sealed writs of passage. The Access Keepers examining each document with practiced efficiency.

But there, there, something moved wrong.

A merchant's writ glowed with the proper authentication sigils, but beneath it, wrapped in layers of illusion so subtle even the Keepers couldn't see, something else pulsed. A presence. Not the merchant at all, but something wearing the merchant's credentials like a stolen coat.

The vision shifted. He saw the merchant enter the city, pass through the gates, move toward the Grand Bazaar where Cassia Goldthread's warehouses stood. And then, darkness. The presence slipped into the shadows between buildings, multiplying, spreading like ink in water.

The Dreaming Tower released him, and he gasped, stumbling against his desk. His hands were shaking. The Tower did not show him every small vulnerability, every minor misconfiguration. It only stirred for threats that could spread. Threats that could corrupt.

He grabbed his staff, a twisted length of dark wood inlaid with glowing script, and descended the spiral stairs three at a time. The Tower's interior shifted around him, shortcuts opening where there had been walls moments before. It wanted him to hurry.

By the time he burst into the streets, he was already composing the message in his mind. The Secret Council would need to convene immediately.

The council chamber sat beneath the Queen's palace, accessible only through a sealed vault door that required three simultaneous keys. The Lorekeeper arrived first, as he always did in emergencies, and waited in the circular stone room lit by floating orbs of pale light. Maps of the Kingdom covered every wall, enchanted to update in real-time as conditions changed.

The Queen arrived next, her silver armor, ceremonial but functional, catching the light. Queen Lyra Brightforge had ruled for fifteen years, guiding the Kingdom through three major corruption events and countless smaller skirmishes. She trusted the Lorekeeper, but he knew that trust was earned anew with each crisis.

"Show me," she said without preamble.

He placed his palm against the central table, and an illusion bloomed above it, a recreation of what the Tower had shown him. The merchant. The hidden presence. The spreading darkness.

"When?" she asked.

"It's happening now, Your Majesty. The breach occurred sometime in the last six hours. The Tower only just detected the anomaly."

High Commander Valdris Ironheart strode in, his heavy boots echoing. He was a broad man with a scarred face and eyes that missed nothing. "How bad?"

"Unknown. But the intrusion came through Merchant's Gate using legitimate credentials."

Valdris swore. "Compromised authentication. That's a Mimic, then. Or worse."

"Or worse," the Lorekeeper agreed.

Merchant-Lord Cassia Goldthread arrived in a swirl of expensive silk, her expression already annoyed. "Please tell me this isn't about shutting down trade again. We're three days from the Harvest Festival. If you close the gates -"

"Someone is already inside, Cassia," the Queen said quietly. "Using credentials that passed every check."

Cassia's expression shifted from annoyance to calculation. "Which merchant house?"

"We don't know yet. The credentials were legitimate, but the entity using them was not."

The vault door opened one final time, and Shade slipped inside like smoke made flesh. He wore his usual dark leathers, his face obscured by a hood enchanted to blur his features. Most people found him unsettling. The Lorekeeper found him necessary.

"You're late," Valdris said.

"I was gathering information," Shade replied, his voice like gravel wrapped in velvet. He produced a small crystal from his pocket and set it on the table. "This is from my contact in the Shadow Markets. Three days ago, a new vendor appeared selling 'master writs', authentication credentials for the Merchant's Gate. Perfect forgeries, supposedly. At least seven were sold before my contact realized what they were."

The Queen's jaw tightened. "Seven."

"I've only detected one intrusion so far," the Lorekeeper said. "But if there are six more -"

"Then we have a coordinated infiltration," Valdris finished. "A planned attack."

Cassia leaned forward, studying the illusion. "That writ belongs to House Meridian. I know because I purchase half their spice imports." Her fingers danced across the table, pulling up additional data from the enchanted maps. "They made a delivery this morning. Seventy-three crates of saffron and black pepper, all logged into my warehouse district."

"Can you verify the delivery was legitimate?" the Queen asked.

"I can have my people check the physical inventory, but that will take hours."

"We don't have hours," the Lorekeeper said. The wrongness in his gut was growing. The Tower had shown him spreading darkness for a reason. "If this entity reached the warehouses, it has access to -"

"To the entire merchant network," Cassia breathed. "Every trade route, every supplier contract, every customer record. Gods below, that's half the Kingdom's commerce."

Valdris was already moving toward the door. "I'll mobilize the Sentinels. We lock down the warehouse district, sweep every -"

"Wait." Shade's voice cut through the room like a blade. "If this is a coordinated attack, the moment you mobilize forces, the other six will know. They'll scatter, burrow deeper, or trigger whatever they came here to do."

"So what do we do?" Valdris growled. "Sit on our hands while they infest the city?"

"Be smart." Shade pulled back his hood just enough to reveal a thin smile. "We know one entry point. We do not alert the others. We track this one, find out what it's doing, and use that to identify the rest."

"That's a significant risk," the Queen said.

"All our options are risky," the Lorekeeper said quietly. "But Shade is right. If we trigger a panic, we lose our chance to understand the full scope of the attack."

Cassia drummed her fingers on the table. "I can give you access to my warehouse logs without raising alarms. If something is moving through my network, we'll see it."

Valdris looked like he wanted to argue, but after a long moment, he nodded. "Fine. But I want Sentinel-Captain Rook positioned with a rapid response team. The moment this goes wrong -"

"The moment it goes wrong, you'll have my blessing to burn it all down," the Queen said. She looked at each of them in turn. "We track, we identify, we eliminate. Agreed?"

"Agreed," they said, almost in unison.

The Lorekeeper returned to the Dreaming Tower and climbed to his observation chamber, a circular room at the Tower's heart where hundreds of floating crystals displayed active monitoring data from across the Kingdom. Each crystal represented a different system, a different stream of information. Normally, they drifted in lazy patterns, their light steady.

Now, three of them pulsed with an angry red glow.

He reached out and touched the first. Immediately, his vision filled with data, warehouse logs from Cassia's merchant network. He could see the transaction records, the authentication tokens, the movement of goods through the supply chain.

And there, buried in the routine traffic, something moved that shouldn't. It was subtle, almost invisible. A series of queries testing access permissions. Probing. Learning. Mapping the network from the inside.

"Reconnaissance," he murmured. "It's not attacking yet. It's preparing."

He touched the second crystal. This one showed him something different, encrypted communications flowing through the Kingdom's message network. The Cipher Monks maintained strict protocols, but he could see anomalies. Packets of data that didn't match standard patterns. They were encrypted with legitimate keys, but the behavior was wrong. Too regular. Too systematic.

The third crystal showed him the Access Keeper logs from Merchant's Gate. He scrolled back through the records, looking for the other six intrusions Shade had mentioned.

It took him an hour to find them. They'd been careful, spacing out their entries over three days, each using a different merchant house's credentials. But now that he knew what to look for, the pattern was obvious. Seven entities, all converging on the Crown City. All granted legitimate access through compromised authentication.

He sent a pulse through his connection to the Tower, and one of the walls in his chamber shimmered, transforming into a map of the city. Seven points of light appeared, scattered across different districts.

One in the warehouse quarter.

Two in the Cloud Spires, where the Kingdom's administrative records were kept.

One near the Cipher Monks' sanctum.

Two in the Outer Provinces' communication hubs.

And one, his blood went cold, one moving toward the Deep Vaults beneath the palace.

He ran.

The Secret Council reconvened within twenty minutes. The Lorekeeper's projection showed them the seven points of light, and he watched their expressions shift from concern to alarm.

"They're not just stealing data," Valdris said. "This is a multi-vector attack. They're positioning for something bigger."

"The Deep Vaults," Cassia said. "What do we keep down there?"

"Citizen records," the Queen said quietly. "Tax information. Land deeds. Census data. Everything."

"And the Cipher Monks' sanctum holds encryption keys," the Lorekeeper added. "If they compromise those -"

"They could decrypt everything we've ever secured," Shade finished. "Every sensitive communication, every protected record, every -"

The Dreaming Tower screamed.

It wasn't a sound. It was a psychic shock that drove the Lorekeeper to his knees. The walls of the council chamber shook, and the enchanted maps flickered. He felt the Tower's panic, its rage, flooding through their connection.

When his vision cleared, he saw it. On the map, the seven points of light had stopped moving.

And they'd started glowing brighter.

"They're activating," he gasped. "Whatever they came here to do, they're doing it now."

Valdris was already shouting orders through his communication crystal. "All forces, Code Crimson! Lock down all entry points, seal the -"

The lights in the chamber went out.

For three heartbeats, they stood in absolute darkness. Then the emergency enchantments activated, bathing the room in eerie red light.

"Report!" the Queen demanded.

A Sentinel burst through the door, breathless. "Your Majesty, we're experiencing cascading failures across the city. The message networks are down, the Cloud Spires' access bridges are offline, and -" He swallowed hard. "- the Deep Vaults' wards just failed. Something got inside."

Shade laughed, a bitter sound. "It's not a theft. It's a corruption event. They're not stealing the data, they're changing it."

The Lorekeeper's mind raced. Corrupted records. Altered citizen data. Modified land deeds. Compromised encryption keys. If this succeeded, the chaos would be catastrophic. The Kingdom wouldn't know who owned what, who owed what, who was what. Trust in every system would collapse.

"We need to cut them off," he said. "Isolate each intrusion point before they can spread further."

"How?" Cassia demanded. "They're inside our most secure systems!"

The Lorekeeper closed his eyes, reaching for the Dreaming Tower's consciousness. The Tower responded immediately, showing him the network of connections between the seven entities. They weren't working independently. They were coordinated, sharing information, supporting each other.

A distributed attack. Like a -

"A Rot Swarm," he breathed. "They're networked together. Take down one, and the others adapt. But if we take them all down simultaneously -"

"We need perfect timing," Valdris said. "Seven separate teams hitting seven targets at exactly the same moment."

"I can coordinate it," the Lorekeeper said. "Through the Tower. It can synchronize the strikes."

The Queen looked at him, her expression grave. "Can you do this?"

He thought of the ancient scripts locked in the Tower's vaults. The dangerous code left by previous Lorekeepers, tools so powerful they were never meant to be used except in the most dire circumstances.

"Yes," he said. "But I'll need to release one of the forbidden protocols. The Purge Script."

The room went silent.

"That hasn't been used in a generation," Cassia whispered.

"Because last time it nearly crashed the entire Kingdom," Valdris added.

"Do it," the Queen said.

The Lorekeeper ascended the Dreaming Tower faster than he'd ever climbed before. The Tower opened passages for him, shortcuts and impossible geometries that let him reach the highest vault in minutes instead of hours.

The vault door was sealed with seven locks, each requiring a different key phrase in the old language. He spoke them rapidly, his voice echoing in the narrow stone passage. The final lock clicked open, and the door swung inward.

Inside, floating in a sphere of amber light, was the Purge Script.

It looked like a scroll, but it was written in code so old and dense that reading it made his eyes water. The previous Lorekeeper had warned him about this tool. It was designed to identify and eliminate hostile entities from the Kingdom's systems, but it was indiscriminate. It would purge everything that didn't match the Kingdom's foundational protocols, including legitimate processes if they'd been modified or corrupted in any way.

Using it meant accepting losses. Accepting that some innocent data, some useful modifications, would be destroyed along with the threat.

But they had no choice.

He lifted the scroll carefully and carried it back to his observation chamber. The Tower was already prepared, its crystals arranged in a perfect circle, each one glowing with the location of one of the seven intrusions.

He unfurled the scroll and began to read, his voice steady despite his racing heart. The words were power made manifest, each syllable activating ancient protocols woven into the Kingdom's very foundation.

Through his connection to the Tower, he felt Valdris's teams moving into position. The Sentinel-Captain at the Deep Vaults. The Wardens at the Cloud Spires. The Cipher Monks preparing emergency containment at their sanctum.

"On my mark," he whispered, and the Tower carried his voice to all of them. "Three. Two. One. Purge."

He spoke the final command, and the scroll erupted with blinding light.

The effect was instantaneous and violent.

In the Deep Vaults, the hostile presence that had been systematically altering citizen records suddenly found itself trapped in a cage of pure logic. The Purge Script recognized it as foreign code, isolated it, and began to dismantle it piece by piece. The entity fought back, throwing everything it had against the containment, but the ancient protocol was relentless. Within seconds, the entity was reduced to inert fragments, then to nothing at all.

The same scene played out across all seven locations. The networked attack, so carefully coordinated, shattered like glass. Each entity died screaming in its own way, bursts of corrupted data, fragments of malicious intent scattering into the void.

But the Purge Script didn't stop there.

It tore through the affected systems, purging anything that had been touched by the intrusion. Records that had been altered were deleted entirely. Processes that had been modified were reset to their original state. The warehouse network's recent transaction history, gone. The Cloud Spires' authentication logs for the past week, erased. The Cipher Monks' working keys, reverted to week-old backups.

The Lorekeeper watched it happen through the Tower's eyes, and his heart ached. They were losing data. Losing work. But they were also surviving.

After five minutes that felt like hours, the script completed its work. The scroll in his hands crumbled to dust, its power spent. The crystals around him dimmed, their angry red glow fading to a soft amber.

The Kingdom was safe.

But wounded.

The Queen stood in the council chamber, reviewing damage reports with a grim expression. Merchant-Lord Cassia sat at the table, her usual composure shaken. High Commander Valdris paced like a caged wolf.

"Seventy-three merchants lost their transaction records," Cassia said quietly. "It'll take weeks to reconstruct them manually. Some may never be fully recovered."

"The Cloud Spires lost eight days of administrative processing," Valdris added. "Applications for land transfers, marriage certificates, business licenses, all gone."

"The Cipher Monks are rebuilding their working keys from backups," the Queen said. "But anything encrypted in the last week will need to be re-encrypted. It's going to cause significant disruption."

The Lorekeeper stood in the corner, exhausted. He'd barely slept in two days. "It could have been worse. If the corruption had spread further -"

"I know," the Queen said. "You did what had to be done."

Shade leaned against the wall, his expression unreadable. "The real question is: who sold those master writs? This wasn't random. Someone targeted our authentication systems specifically."

"My contacts in the Shadow Markets are pursuing that," he continued. "But whoever orchestrated this covered their tracks well. It'll take time."

"Then we'll take the time," the Queen said. "But in the meantime, we need to prevent this from happening again."

She looked at the Lorekeeper. "I want a full review of our authentication protocols. If legitimate credentials can be forged that easily, we need to redesign the system from the ground up."

He nodded. "I'll work with the Access Keepers and the Cipher Monks. We'll need to implement multi-layered verification. The writs alone clearly aren't enough."

"And I want my warehouses on a rotating audit schedule," Cassia said. "If something infiltrates my network again, I want to know within hours, not days."

"Agreed," Valdris said. "And I'm doubling patrols at all entry points. Merchant's Gate gets tripled. No one gets in without a physical inspection in addition to credential verification."

The Queen looked at each of them in turn. "This attack failed because we worked together. Because the Lorekeeper trusted his instincts, because Shade brought us intelligence, because Cassia gave us access, and because Valdris's forces executed perfectly." She paused. "But it was close. Too close."

"They'll try again," Shade said. "Different tactics, but they'll try. They always do."

"Let them," the Queen said, and her voice was iron. "We'll be ready."

Late that night, the Lorekeeper sat in his chamber at the top of the Dreaming Tower. The city below sparkled with enchanted lights, peaceful and oblivious to how close it had come to chaos.

The Tower hummed around him, content now that the threat was eliminated. He placed his hand on the stone wall, feeling the ancient consciousness that dwelled within.

"Thank you," he whispered.

The Tower didn't respond in words. It never did. But he felt its acknowledgment, warm and certain.

A soft knock at his door interrupted his meditation. He turned to find Junior Archivist Pip standing in the doorway, looking nervous.

"Master Lorekeeper? I... I heard what happened. I wanted to ask if there was anything I could do to help with the recovery efforts."

The Lorekeeper studied the young man. Pip was eager, competent when properly supervised, and desperately wanted to prove himself. He was also the same archivist who'd accidentally created a cascading update failure last month by optimizing a scheduling script without testing it first.

But he was also willing to learn.

"Actually, yes," the Lorekeeper said. "I need someone to help me document this incident for the Chronicles. Every detail, every decision, every mistake. So that the next Lorekeeper, or the next generation, can learn from what we did right and what we did wrong."

Pip's face lit up. "I can do that! I'll start immediately -"

"Tomorrow," the Lorekeeper said firmly. "Tonight, we rest. We've earned it."

After Pip left, the Lorekeeper turned back to the window. Somewhere out there, in the shadows beyond the Kingdom's walls, the entities responsible for this attack were planning their next move. And somewhere in the Shadow Markets, Shade was hunting them.

The Lorekeeper would record this incident in the Chronicles, as he'd recorded dozens before it. The tale would join the others in the Tower's vast archives, a warning and a lesson for future defenders.

But he knew the truth that every Lorekeeper learned eventually: the threats never truly stopped. They evolved. They adapted. They learned from their failures just as the Kingdom learned from its victories.

The eternal dance of security and threat would continue, turning like the great wheel of the stars above.

And the Dreaming Tower would keep watch, as it always had.

As it always would.

Story Summary

Seven coordinated attackers used forged but legitimate-looking credentials to infiltrate the Kingdom through trusted merchant access points. They positioned themselves across critical infrastructure not to steal, but to corrupt records and keys. The Lorekeeper identified the pattern as a Rot Swarm, a distributed attack. The Kingdom invoked a dangerous Purge Script, accepting loss to stop the spread.

Real-World Cybersecurity Concept

Supply Chain Attack and Third-Party Vendor Compromise

Real-World Examples

  • SolarWinds (2020 to 2021): A trusted update mechanism was compromised. The malicious update appeared legitimate and enabled lateral movement across many organizations.
  • 3CX Supply Chain Attack (2023): Malicious code was inserted into a build pipeline and distributed through legitimate updates to a large customer base.
  • NotPetya (2017) via M.E.Doc: A poisoned vendor update delivered destructive malware that spread broadly and caused massive global damage.

Real-World Lesson for Non-Technical Readers

Imagine you order supplies from a vendor you have trusted for years. Their trucks look right. Their invoices look right. Their paperwork checks out. But somewhere in their process, an attacker swapped the legitimate goods with defective ones. You install those trusted supplies into your operations, and the damage begins from the inside.

This is what supply chain attacks look like. The vendor is real, but the pathway is poisoned. Because the relationship is trusted, organizations often scrutinize it less than they would an unknown source.

What This Means for Your Organization

For Enterprises

  • Vendor risk management: Trust is not a control. Even highly reputable vendors can become the entry point.
  • Verification for updates: Legitimate updates still deserve staged rollout and validation.
  • Segmentation: Isolate systems so compromise in one zone does not automatically cascade.
  • Detection over prevention: You may not stop every initial entry, but you can reduce dwell time.
  • Incident response readiness: Know your “Purge Script” choices, including what you can afford to lose to stop spread.

For Small Businesses

  • Know your vendors: Who has access to your systems and what tools auto-update?
  • Backups: If you must wipe systems, can you restore quickly and confidently?
  • MFA: Stolen credentials do less damage when another factor is required.
  • Watch for weird behavior: If a tool starts touching data it never touched before, investigate.
  • Have a response plan: Decide who you call and what you shut off when trust is broken.

The Uncomfortable Truth

The Merchant’s Gate breach succeeded because attackers used legitimate credentials through trusted pathways. No amount of wall-building stops that. You cannot simply “firewall harder.” You must assume trusted relationships will be exploited and build verification, monitoring, segmentation, and response plans accordingly.

Cost of Inaction

Organizations that treat vendor trust as a substitute for verification often discover the weakness too late. When the supply chain is the attack vector, the damage is not just technical. It hits operations, customer trust, and survivability.
