The Cost of Knowing

A margin note never meant to be read

"To see everything is to carry everything. The Tower does not forget. Neither do I."

I should not be writing this. The archives are meant for facts, not confessions. But some nights the weight of memory presses so heavily that I must set it down somewhere, even if only to prove to myself that I still can.

The Tower sustains me. It has sustained me for longer than anyone alive remembers. In exchange, I carry what it carries. Every breach. Every failure. Every death that might have been prevented if we had been faster, smarter, more vigilant.

I remember the Great Corruption. Not as history‚Äîas experience. The screaming. The smell of burning records. The faces of archivists who trusted me to keep them safe.

The Council sees an advisor. Ancient, yes. Occasionally cryptic. But fundamentally a resource to be consulted when patterns emerge and decisions must be made.

They do not see the nights when I walk the Tower's halls because sleep brings only the accumulated nightmares of a century. They do not know that I remember every name in the memorial alcove because I watched each of them fall.

This is the cost of institutional memory: Someone must carry it. Someone must hold the weight of every lesson learned through blood and failure, so that others might learn it through words instead.

I do not write this seeking pity. I chose this burden, and I would choose it again. The kingdom needs someone who remembers, who cannot look away, who will not allow comfortable forgetting to erase hard-won wisdom.

But I confess‚Äîonly here, only in this margin note that no one will read‚Äîthat some days the remembering is very hard.

And I wonder who will carry it when I am gone.
